Subhash Ramanna Sonnad

November 13, 1932 — July 12, 2021

Subhash Sonnad was born in 1932 in his mother's village of Yadhalli in the state of Karnataka. He was only 4 lbs at birth, and weak enough that many thought he might not survive. Despite this his father was very happy that he had his first son.

Because there were no schools in his village, his father moved to a nearby village, so all the children could go to school, and made arrangements for a variety of teachers to come and teach in both Kannada and English.

After moving to Bagalkot, Subhash excelled at academics and by the time he was 10 he was testing at the 10th grade level, soon passing the 11th grade exam. After this he entered college, and finished in 4 years. However, he faced many adversities due to his age and small physique. But he persevered and graduated with degrees in economics, political science, and law at the age of only 16. However, he was then prevented from immediately advancing further, because it was determined he was too young.

Though he had many potential opportunities in government and engineering as well as in the field of law, which his father wanted him to take up, Subhash had other plans. He was determined to pursue a course in Sociology, which he felt was the key to understanding and improving society, and understanding the condition of rural farmers. He then moved to what was then Bombay and pursued his masters degree in this field.

After receiving this degree Subhash decided to pursue academics in the United States, and gained admission to the University of Wisconsin in Madison. In Wisconsin, he met his future wife, Jayashree, and they had their first child, Seema. After graduating, he secured a teaching job at Western Michigan University in Kalamazoo, and had their second child Rahul.

When they were young, Subhash was a prolific storyteller often telling tales of Bhima, and other Indian legends.

With all his schooling, Subhash was the guiding spirit of the Sonnad Family with regards to pursuit of higher education. Later in life, the family (which grew to 12 siblings) would be cited in the Guinness World Book of Records as the family with the most PhDs.

Subhash became one of the most loved personalities in the Sociology Department at WMU, amongst both students and faculty, and expanded his teaching into the fields of computers, business, and criminal law. He passed his love of academics onto his daughter and son through continual studies with them, and viewed their admission to Stanford and Harvard respectively as great accomplishments.

Subhash spent summers with his family touring the United States in a VW microbus, camping at all the national parks, and visiting 48 of 50 states. He was not afraid to jump into the ice cold waters of the mountains.

Subhash continued teaching well into his late 70s and was befriended by a huge number of students at the university, many of whom he would host at his house in Kalamazoo.

After retiring, Subhash moved to California, near his son, daughter-in-law and three grandchildren. For his final year Subhash resided next to the Pacific Ocean on the west coast of Oahu, Hawaii with his son and then finally in Austin, Texas in the company of three Alpaca.

Subhash was a continual inspiration to those around him due to his thoughtfulness, his advanced sense of humor, and his kind heart.
